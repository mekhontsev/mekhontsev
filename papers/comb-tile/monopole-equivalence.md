# Monopole Equivalence Theorem (Draft)

## Setup

Let $\{A_n\}_{n \geq n_0}$ be a family of disk-like self-similar tiles in $\mathbb{R}^2$, each generated by $n$ contractions of ratio $1/\sqrt{n}$, satisfying:

- the number of edge neighbors $|\mathcal{A}|$ is independent of $n$ (bounded neighbor complexity); write $K := |\mathcal{A}|$,
- $\dim_H(\partial A_n) < 2$ for each $n$ and $\dim_H(\partial A_n) \to 2$ as $n \to \infty$,
- the boundary substitution matrix $M_n$ is primitive for all sufficiently large $n$.

Let $\lambda_1 = \rho(M_n)$ be the Perron eigenvalue and let $\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_d$ be the remaining nonzero eigenvalues ($d \le K$). Define

$$c \;=\; \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\operatorname{tr}(M_n)}{n}.$$

For the direction $c=1 \Rightarrow \rho_n\to 0$, we impose the following explicit structural hypothesis.

**Monopole matrix condition (MMC).** There exists an index $a$ such that

- $(M_n)_{aa} = n - 1 + O(1)$,
- every other entry of $M_n$ is $O(1)$.

Equivalently: there is at most one $\Theta(n)$ entry of $M_n$; if it exists, it is diagonal, and all other entries are uniformly bounded.

## Main Result

**Theorem (Monopole Equivalence, with MMC in the $c=1$ direction).** Under the setup above:

**(i)** If $\rho_n := |\lambda_2|/\lambda_1 \to 0$, then $c=1$.

**(ii)** If $c=1$ and MMC holds, then $\rho_n \to 0$.

Moreover:

### Case $c = 1$ with MMC (monopole regime)

$$\lambda_1 = n - 1 + O(1), \qquad \lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_d = O(1), \qquad \rho_n = O(1/n).$$

Hence

$$2 - \dim_H(\partial A_n) = \frac{2\log(n/\lambda_1)}{\log n} = O\!\left(\frac{1}{n\log n}\right).$$

### Case $c \ge 2$ (competing regime)

$$\rho_n \;\ge\; \frac{c-1}{c}+o(1)\;>\;0,$$

and

$$2 - \dim_H(\partial A_n) = \Theta\!\left(\frac{1}{n\log n}\right).$$

So the spectral gap does not close in the competing regime.

---

## Proof sketch

### $(ii) \Rightarrow (i)$

Assume $c=1$ and MMC. Write

$$M_n = \alpha_n e_a e_a^T + B_n, \qquad \alpha_n := (M_n)_{aa} = n-1+O(1).$$

By MMC, every entry of $B_n$ is $O(1)$. Since $K$ is fixed, $\|B_n\|=O(1)$ in any matrix norm.

The matrix $\alpha_n e_a e_a^T$ has one eigenvalue $\alpha_n$ and $K-1$ zero eigenvalues. Therefore $M_n$ is a rank-1 dominant term plus a bounded perturbation. By standard eigenvalue perturbation (equivalently, by Gershgorin discs), one eigenvalue satisfies

$$\lambda_1 = \alpha_n + O(1) = n-1+O(1),$$

while all other eigenvalues remain bounded:

$$\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_d = O(1).$$

Hence

$$\rho_n = \frac{|\lambda_2|}{\lambda_1} = O(1/n) \to 0.$$

(For comparison: a Frobenius bound from row sums only gives $|\lambda_2|=O(n)$, which is insufficient; MMC is the extra structure that forces $|\lambda_2|=O(1)$.)

### $(i) \Rightarrow (ii)$

If $\rho_n \to 0$ then $|\lambda_i| = o(n)$ for all $i \geq 2$, so

$$\lambda_1 = \operatorname{tr}(M_n) - \sum_{i \geq 2}\lambda_i = cn + o(n).$$

Since $\lambda_1 < n$ (because $\dim_H(\partial A_n) < 2$), we get $c \leq 1$.

If $c = 0$ then $\sum_{i \geq 2}\lambda_i = -n + O(1)$ and $|\lambda_2| \geq n/(d-1)$, contradicting $\rho_n \to 0$.

Hence $c = 1$. $\square$

### Convergence rates

Under $c=1$ with MMC, $\lambda_1 = n-1+O(1)$, so

$$2-s_n = \frac{2\log(n/\lambda_1)}{\log n} = O\!\left(\frac{1}{n\log n}\right).$$

The sharper estimate $\lambda_1 = n - O(1/n)$ does not follow from $c=1$ alone. It is specific to the comb family and comes from its quartic characteristic equation $p_n(\lambda)=0$ evaluated at $\lambda=\lambda_1$.

For $c\ge 2$, one still has $n-\lambda_1=\Theta(1)$, hence

$$2-s_n = \Theta\!\left(\frac{1}{n\log n}\right).$$

---

## Corollary (optimality of monopole mechanism)

Among families where MMC holds at $c=1$, the monopole mechanism forces a closing spectral gap ($\rho_n\to 0$), while the competing regime $c\ge 2$ cannot close the gap ($\rho_n\not\to 0$).

For the comb family, the quartic structure improves the generic MMC rate and gives the faster asymptotic

$$2-\dim_H(\partial A_n) \sim \frac{8}{n^2\log n}.$$

---

## Remark (for the comb family)

For the comb family, MMC is verified directly from the boundary substitution matrix (equation (3.7) in the article):

$$(M_n)_{66}=n-1$$

is the only $\Theta(n)$ entry, and all other entries are $O(1)$. Therefore the abstract $c=1+\text{MMC}$ argument applies, and then the quartic $p_n$ gives the sharper expansion $\lambda_1=n-4/n+O(1/n^2)$ and $\rho_n = 1/\sqrt{n}+O(n^{-3/2})$.

---

## Model example ($c = 2$, competing regime)

Consider the $4 \times 4$ primitive matrix

$$M_n = \begin{pmatrix} n-1 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & n-1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

with $\operatorname{tr}(M_n) = 2(n-1)$, so $c = 2$. By symmetry the eigenvalues split into a symmetric sector (eigenvector $(1,1,x,x)$) with $\lambda^2 - (n-1)\lambda - 4 = 0$, giving

$$\lambda_1 = n - 1 + 4/n + O(1/n^2),$$

and an antisymmetric sector (eigenvector $(1,-1,0,0)$) with

$$\lambda_2 = n - 1.$$

Therefore

$$\rho_n = \frac{n-1}{n - 1 + 4/n} = 1 - \frac{4}{n(n-1)} \longrightarrow 1,$$

confirming no monopole structure, and

$$2 - \dim_H \sim \frac{2}{n \log n},$$

which is slower than the comb family's $8/(n^2 \log n)$ by a factor of $n/4$.

## Open Question

Does there exist a disk-like family with bounded neighbor complexity, $\dim_H(\partial A_n) \to 2$, and $c \geq 2$?

By the theorem above, such a family would necessarily have:

- $\rho_n \not\to 0$ (no monopole spectral concentration),
- convergence only of order $2 - \dim_H = \Theta(1/(n\log n))$,
- multiple boundary pieces carrying comparable $\mathcal{H}^s$-measure.
