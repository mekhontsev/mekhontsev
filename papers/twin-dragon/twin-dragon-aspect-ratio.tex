\begin{abstract}
We show that the geometric aspect ratio of the Twin Dragon equals $1/\varphi$,
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.
The result follows by solving the covariance fixed-point equation for the
self-similar measure, which coincides with Lebesgue area since the
similarity dimension equals~$2$ and the open set condition holds.
The appearance of $\varphi$ is surprising: the Twin Dragon is defined
purely via the Gaussian integer $1+i$, with no pentagonal or Fibonacci
structure in its construction.
More generally, we derive a closed-form expression for the aspect ratio of
the entire one-parameter family $\{f_{1,2}(z)=a(z\pm 1)\}$ for arbitrary
$a\in\mathbb{C}$ with $|a|<1$.
The same constant $\varphi$ also governs the orientation: the major axis
of the Twin Dragon makes angle $-\arctan\varphi\approx -58.3^\circ$
with the horizontal.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Twin Dragon is a classical plane-filling fractal \cite{DavisKnuth1970} governed
by the Gaussian integer $1+i$: its two defining contractions share the linear part
$(1-i)/2$, and their translations are symmetric about the origin.
Despite this simple, purely arithmetic construction, the attractor has a non-trivial
shape that is not immediately obvious from the definition.
Visually the Twin Dragon looks somewhat elongated --- neither a square nor a thin
sliver --- which raises the question of its precise aspect ratio.

A natural quantitative measure of shape for a planar set is the \emph{geometric aspect
ratio}: the ratio of standard deviations along the principal axes of its area
distribution, or equivalently the square root of the ratio of the eigenvalues of the
second-moment (covariance) matrix $M$ of the uniform measure on the attractor.
This definition gives the expected answers for elementary shapes: a circle has aspect
ratio~$1$ (all eigenvalues equal); a $p \times q$ rectangle has aspect ratio $p/q$
(eigenvalues $p^2/12$ and $q^2/12$); and a square has aspect ratio~$1$.

The approach of characterising IFS attractors through moments of their invariant
measures was introduced by Vrscay and Roehrig \cite{VrscayRoehrig1989} and
systematically developed in \cite{Vrscay1991}.  The key observation is that the
self-similar measure satisfies a linear fixed-point equation in its moments, which
can be solved exactly.  In favourable cases --- when the similarity dimension equals
the ambient dimension --- this gives the moments of the actual area measure on the
attractor.

We apply this method to the Twin Dragon.  The covariance matrix turns out to be
$M = \tfrac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\-1&3\end{pmatrix}$,
whose eigenvalues involve $\sqrt{5}$, leading to the aspect ratio $1/\varphi$,
where $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.  The appearance of $\varphi$
in a fractal with no pentagonal geometry is unexpected.
Although the method of moments for IFS goes back to \cite{VrscayRoehrig1989},
the closed-form formula for the aspect ratio of the family $\{a(z\pm 1)\}$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:family} below) is, to our knowledge, new.

\textit{Discovery.}
The result was first found empirically using the IFStile software
\cite{Mekhontsev2019}, which implements the moment invariants of
\cite{VrscayRoehrig1989,Vrscay1991} as part of its algebraic IFS search engine.
Among the similarity invariants reported by IFStile for the Twin Dragon, the
aspect ratio $1/\varphi$ appeared as a numerical value; the present paper supplies
the exact proof and its generalisation to the full one-parameter family
$\{a(z\pm 1) : a\in\mathbb{C},\,|a|<1\}$.
This is an instance of \emph{experimental mathematics} \cite{BorweinBailey2004}:
a computer experiment suggests a precise conjecture, which is then confirmed
by a rigorous proof.

The paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:ifs} recalls the definition of the Twin Dragon and its IFS.
Section~\ref{sec:family} derives a closed-form aspect ratio formula for the
full one-parameter family $\{f_{1,2}(z) = a(z\pm 1) : a\in\mathbb{C},\,|a|<1\}$.
Section~\ref{sec:td} applies the formula to the Twin Dragon and proves the
main theorem.

\section{The IFS}\label{sec:ifs}

An iterated function system (IFS) \cite{Hutchinson1981} is a finite collection of
contractions on a complete metric space; by Hutchinson's theorem there exists a
unique non-empty compact attractor.  For background on self-similar sets and
fractal geometry see \cite{Falconer2014}.

The Twin Dragon $A \subset \mathbb{C}$ is the attractor of the IFS \cite{DavisKnuth1970}
\begin{equation}
  A = f_1(A) \cup f_2(A),
  \qquad
  f_{1,2}(z) = \frac{1-i}{2}(z \pm 1).
\end{equation}
Both maps have the same contraction ratio $r = |{(1-i)/2}| = 1/\sqrt{2}$,
so the similarity dimension is $\alpha = -\log 2 / \log r = 2$:
the attractor has positive area.

\section{The general family}\label{sec:family}

Consider the two-map IFS $\{f_{1,2}(z) = a(z \pm 1)\}$ with equal weights
$\tfrac{1}{2}$, for an arbitrary parameter $a \in \mathbb{C}$ with $|a| < 1$.
The Twin Dragon is the special case $a = (1-i)/2$, studied in
Section~\ref{sec:td}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:family}
Let $a \in \mathbb{C}$ with $|a| < 1$.  The
invariant (equal-weight) measure $\mu_a$ of the IFS
$\{f_{1,2}(z) = a(z\pm 1)\}$ has covariance eigenvalues
\[
  I_{1,2} = \frac{|a|^2}{2}\left(\frac{1}{1-|a|^2} \mp \frac{1}{|1-a^2|}\right)
\]
and aspect ratio
\begin{equation}\label{eq:family}
  \mathrm{AR}^2 = \frac{|1-a^2| - (1-|a|^2)}{|1-a^2| + (1-|a|^2)}.
\end{equation}
In particular, $\mathrm{AR}=0$ if and only if $a$ is real.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $Z$ be a random variable with distribution $\mu_a$; the fixed-point
equation $\mu_a = \tfrac{1}{2}(f_1)_*\mu_a + \tfrac{1}{2}(f_2)_*\mu_a$
becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fp}
  E[h(Z)] = \tfrac{1}{2}E[h(aZ+a)] + \tfrac{1}{2}E[h(aZ-a)]
\end{equation}
for every integrable $h$.  Applying \eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z)=z$ gives
$E[Z] = aE[Z]$, hence $E[Z]=0$ since $|a|<1$.
Applying \eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z) = z^2$ and expanding:
\[
  E[Z^2] = a^2 E[Z^2] + a^2,
  \qquad \text{so} \qquad
  E[Z^2] = \frac{a^2}{1-a^2}.
\]
Applying \eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z) = |z|^2$:
\[
  E[|Z|^2] = |a|^2(E[|Z|^2] + 1),
  \qquad \text{so} \qquad
  E[|Z|^2] = \frac{|a|^2}{1-|a|^2}.
\]
For $Z = X+iY$, the covariance matrix has
$\operatorname{tr}M = E[|Z|^2]$ and
\[
  \det M = \tfrac{1}{4}\bigl(E[|Z|^2]^2 - |E[Z^2]|^2\bigr).
\]
The eigenvalue discriminant is therefore
\[
  (\operatorname{tr}M)^2 - 4\det M
  = \bigl|E[Z^2]\bigr|^2
  = \frac{|a|^4}{|1-a^2|^2},
\]
giving the eigenvalues in the statement.  The inequality
$|1-a^2| \geq 1-|a|^2$ (triangle inequality) ensures $I_1 \geq 0$,
with equality iff $a$ is real.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:bound}
Formula~\eqref{eq:family} shows that $\mathrm{AR}$ depends on $a$ only
through $(|a|^2, |1-a^2|)$: the attractor is nearly round when $|1-a^2|$
is large relative to $1-|a|^2$, and degenerates to a segment when
$|1-a^2|\approx 1-|a|^2$ (i.e.\ $a$ nearly real).
For every $a$ in the family, $\mathrm{AR}(a) \leq |a|$, with equality
if and only if $a$ is purely imaginary.
Indeed, $|1-a^2| \leq 1+|a|^2$ (triangle inequality), with equality iff
$a^2/|a^2|=-1$, i.e.\ $a\in i\mathbb{R}$.
Substituting $|1-a^2|=1+|a|^2$ into~\eqref{eq:family} gives
$\mathrm{AR}^2 = |a|^2$.
In particular, for the plane-filling sub-family $|a|=1/\sqrt{2}$
(similarity dimension~$2$), the maximum $\mathrm{AR}=1/\sqrt{2}$ is
achieved by the IFS $\{(i/\sqrt{2})(z\pm 1)\}$, whose attractor is
the rectangle $[-1,1]\times[-\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2}]$ (verified by checking
that $f_1$ and $f_2$ map it to the top and bottom halves respectively).
This rectangle tiles the plane by translation along $[2,0]$ and $[0,2\sqrt{2}]$.
Among named tiles in this sub-family, the Twin Dragon
($a=(1-i)/2$, an algebraic integer satisfying $2a^2-2a+1=0$) gives
$\mathrm{AR}=1/\varphi\approx 0.618$.
Figure~\ref{fig:ar} shows $\mathrm{AR}$ over the entire sub-family.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
A second named tile in the sub-family $|a|=1/\sqrt{2}$ is the
\emph{tame twindragon} \cite[p.~550]{Bandt1991}, the attractor of $\{f_{1,2}(z)=a(z\pm 1)\}$
with $a=(1-i\sqrt{7})/4$.
One computes $1-a^2=(11+i\sqrt{7})/8$, hence $|1-a^2|=\sqrt{2}$, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:family} gives
\[
  \mathrm{AR}^2 = \frac{\sqrt{2}-\tfrac{1}{2}}{\sqrt{2}+\tfrac{1}{2}}
               = \frac{2\sqrt{2}-1}{2\sqrt{2}+1}
               = \frac{9-4\sqrt{2}}{7}
               \approx 0.478,
  \qquad
  \mathrm{AR} \approx 0.691.
\]
This tile arises as the numeration digit set for
the complex base $\beta=(1+i\sqrt{7})/2$
(satisfying $\beta^2-\beta+2=0$ and $|\beta|^2=2$)
via $a=1/\beta$;\footnote{The attractor is the set
$\bigl\{\sum_{k\geq 1}d_k\beta^{-k}:d_k\in\{-1,+1\}\bigr\}\subset\mathbb{C}$.}
it is marked in Figure~\ref{fig:ar}.
\end{remark}

\section{The Twin Dragon}\label{sec:td}

For the Twin Dragon the similarity dimension equals the ambient
dimension $\alpha = 2$; the open set condition holds, so by
\cite[\S5]{Hutchinson1981} the self-similar measure coincides with
Lebesgue area on the attractor, and the covariance matrix $M$ describes
the actual geometric shape (Figure~\ref{fig:td}).

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=6cm]{twin-dragon.png}
\caption{The Twin Dragon with its $1.5$-sigma principal-axis ellipse
(red).  The major axis lies along $(1,-\varphi)$ and the minor axis
along $(\varphi,1)$; their ratio of lengths equals the aspect ratio
$\mathrm{AR}=1/\varphi\approx 0.618$.}
\label{fig:td}
\end{figure}

Substituting $a = (1-i)/2$ into Theorem~\ref{thm:family}:
$|a|^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $a^2 = -\tfrac{i}{2}$,
$1-a^2 = \tfrac{2+i}{2}$, so
\[
  E[Z^2] = \frac{a^2}{1-a^2} = \frac{-i}{2+i} = \frac{-1-2i}{5},
  \qquad
  E[|Z|^2] = \frac{|a|^2}{1-|a|^2} = 1.
\]
The covariance matrix follows via
$M_{11}=\tfrac{1}{2}(E[|Z|^2]+\mathrm{Re}\,E[Z^2])$,
$M_{22}=\tfrac{1}{2}(E[|Z|^2]-\mathrm{Re}\,E[Z^2])$,
$M_{12}=\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Im}\,E[Z^2]$:
\begin{equation}
  M = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}2 & -1 \\ -1 & 3\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The matrix $M$ has $\operatorname{tr} M = 1$ and $\det M = \frac{1}{5}$,
so its eigenvalues are
\begin{equation}
  I_{1,2} = \frac{1 \mp \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{5}}}{2}.
\end{equation}
The geometric aspect ratio --- the ratio of standard deviations along
the principal axes of $M$ --- is
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{\frac{I_1}{I_2}}
  = \sqrt{\frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{\sqrt{5}+1}}
  = \sqrt{\frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}}
  = \frac{1}{\varphi},
\end{equation}
where $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.

\begin{corollary}
The attractor of the IFS $\bigl\{f_{1,2}(z) = \tfrac{1-i}{2}(z \pm 1)\bigr\}$
has covariance matrix $M = \tfrac{1}{5}\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}2&-1\\-1&3\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$
and geometric aspect ratio $1/\varphi = (\sqrt{5}-1)/2 \approx 0.618$,
where $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}
The Twin Dragon is defined by the Gaussian integer $1+i$, with no
pentagon, decagon, or Fibonacci recurrence in the construction.
The emergence of $\varphi$ is therefore unexpected.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The golden ratio likewise governs the \emph{orientation} of the Twin Dragon.
For $Z = X + iY$ with $E[Z] = 0$, writing $E[Z^2] = \rho e^{2i\psi}$
($\rho > 0$), the entries $M_{11} = \tfrac{1}{2}(E[|Z|^2] + \mathrm{Re}\,E[Z^2])$,
$M_{22} = \tfrac{1}{2}(E[|Z|^2] - \mathrm{Re}\,E[Z^2])$,
$M_{12} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Im}\,E[Z^2]$ give
\[
  M = \frac{E[|Z|^2]}{2}\,I
    + \frac{\rho}{2}
      \begin{pmatrix}\cos 2\psi & \sin 2\psi \\ \sin 2\psi & -\cos 2\psi\end{pmatrix},
\]
whose larger eigenvalue has eigenvector $(\cos\psi, \sin\psi)$.
Hence the major axis of any member of the family $\{a(z\pm 1)\}$ makes angle
\[
  \psi = \tfrac{1}{2}\arg\!\left(\tfrac{a^2}{1-a^2}\right)
\]
with the positive $x$-axis.
For the Twin Dragon, $E[Z^2] = (-1-2i)/5$, so $2\psi = \arg(-1-2i)$
lies in the third quadrant with $\tan(2\psi) = 2$.
Applying the double-angle identity with $\psi\in(-\tfrac{\pi}{2},-\tfrac{\pi}{4})$,
one gets $\tan\psi = (-1-\sqrt{5})/2 = -\varphi$.
Thus the major axis has direction vector $(1, -\varphi)$, making angle
$-\arctan\varphi \approx -58.3^\circ$ with the horizontal,
and the same constant $\varphi$ governs both the aspect ratio
($\mathrm{AR} = 1/\varphi$) and the orientation ($\tan\psi = -\varphi$).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The number $5$ --- and hence $\varphi$ --- can be traced to a single
Gaussian prime.  By the ergodic theorem for random iteration
\cite{BarnsleyElton1988}, $\mu$ is the distribution of the random series
\[
  Z = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon_k\,\lambda^k,
  \qquad \lambda = \tfrac{1-i}{2},
  \qquad \varepsilon_k \overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} \{\pm 1\},
\]
so $E[|Z|^2] = 1$ and
\[
  E[Z^2]
  = \frac{\lambda^2}{1-\lambda^2}
  = \frac{-i/2}{(2+i)/2}
  = \frac{-1-2i}{5}.
\]
The ellipticity $\rho = |E[Z^2]| = 1/\sqrt{5}$ controls the
eigenvalue spread: $I_{1,2} = (1\pm\rho)/2$.
The value $5 = \mathbf{N}(2+i)$ arises as the Gaussian norm of $2+i$,
which appears because $1-\lambda^2 = (2+i)/2$.
Substituting $\rho=1/\sqrt{5}$:
\[
  \frac{I_1}{I_2}
  = \frac{1-\rho}{1+\rho}
  = \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{\sqrt{5}+1}
  = \frac{1}{\varphi^2},
\]
where the last step uses $\varphi^2=\varphi+1$.
Thus $\varphi$ enters via the Gaussian factorisation $5=(2+i)(2-i)$
in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$.
\end{remark}
