\begin{abstract}
We show that the geometric aspect ratio of the Twin Dragon
--- defined via the Gaussian integer $1+i$ ---
equals $1/\varphi$, where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio,
and that its major axis makes angle $-\arctan\varphi$ with the horizontal.
More generally, for matrix IFS $\{f_k(\mathbf{v})=AR_k(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{t}_k)\}$
with Jordan-basis isometries $R_k$ and centred translations, the aspect ratio
is given by a closed-form formula depending on the eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $A$
and a single anisotropy parameter $\kappa_\varepsilon$; this result extends to
IFS where some $R_k$ are reflections in the Jordan basis
(Theorem~\ref{thm:mixed}), and the classical scalar
formula for $\{f_k(z)=a(z+t_k)\}$ is a special case (Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}).
When $a=1/\lambda$ for a Gaussian integer~$\lambda$, the aspect ratio
lies in a quadratic field determined by $\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2\!-\!1)$.
Every metallic ratio $\mu_m=(m+\sqrt{m^2+4})/2$ $(m\geq 1)$
arises as the reciprocal aspect ratio of a plane-filling tile
over~$\mathbb{Z}[i]$;
moreover, $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is the unique imaginary quadratic ring
where collinear digits can produce metallic-ratio aspect ratios.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Twin Dragon is a classical plane-filling fractal \cite{DavisKnuth1970} governed
by the Gaussian integer $1+i$: its two defining contractions share the linear part
$(1-i)/2$, and their translations are symmetric about the origin.
Despite this simple, purely arithmetic construction, the attractor has a non-trivial
shape that is not immediately obvious from the definition.
Visually the Twin Dragon looks somewhat elongated --- neither a square nor a thin
sliver --- which raises the question of its precise aspect ratio.

A natural quantitative measure of shape for a planar set is the \emph{geometric aspect
ratio}: the ratio of standard deviations along the principal axes of its area
distribution, or equivalently the square root of the ratio of the eigenvalues of the
second-moment (covariance) matrix $M$ of the uniform measure on the attractor.
This definition gives the expected answers for elementary shapes:
an ellipse with semi-axes $\alpha \geq \beta > 0$ has aspect ratio $\beta/\alpha$
(eigenvalues $\alpha^2/4$ and $\beta^2/4$, so the definition directly recovers the
ratio of the semi-axes); a $p \times q$ rectangle has aspect ratio $p/q$
(eigenvalues $p^2/12$ and $q^2/12$); and a square has aspect ratio~$1$.

The approach of characterising IFS attractors through moments of their invariant
measures was introduced by Vrscay and Roehrig \cite{VrscayRoehrig1989} and
systematically developed in \cite{Vrscay1991}.  The key observation is that the
self-similar measure satisfies a linear fixed-point equation in its moments, which
can be solved exactly.  When the open set condition holds, Hutchinson's theorem
\cite{Hutchinson1981} guarantees that the self-similar measure coincides with the
normalised Hausdorff measure $\mathcal{H}^s|_A$, so the covariance matrix captures
the actual geometry of the attractor at whatever dimension $s$ it carries.  In the
special case where the similarity dimension $s$ equals the ambient dimension,
$\mathcal{H}^s$ reduces to Lebesgue area.

We apply this method to the Twin Dragon.  The covariance matrix turns out to be
$M = \tfrac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\-1&3\end{pmatrix}$,
whose eigenvalues involve $\sqrt{5}$, leading to the aspect ratio $1/\varphi$,
where $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.  The appearance of $\varphi$
in a fractal with no pentagonal geometry is unexpected.
Although the method of moments for IFS goes back to \cite{VrscayRoehrig1989},
the closed-form aspect ratio formula for matrix IFS with Jordan-basis
isometries (Theorem~\ref{thm:family} below), its extension to IFS with
mixed rotations and reflections (Theorem~\ref{thm:mixed}), with the scalar
case recovered as Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}, and the
characterisation of its quadratic field for Gaussian-integer parameters
(Corollary~\ref{cor:field}) are, to our knowledge, new.

\textit{Discovery.}
The result was first found empirically using the IFStile software
\cite{Mekhontsev2019}, which implements the moment invariants of
\cite{VrscayRoehrig1989,Vrscay1991} as part of its algebraic IFS search engine.
Among the similarity invariants reported by IFStile for the Twin Dragon, the
aspect ratio $1/\varphi$ appeared as a numerical value; the present paper supplies
the exact proof and its generalisation to matrix IFS
$\{f_k(\mathbf{v})=AR_k(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{t}_k)\}$ with Jordan-basis isometries,
with the scalar case $\{f_k(z)=a(z+t_k)\}$ as a special case.
This is an instance of \emph{experimental mathematics} \cite{BorweinBailey2004}:
a computer experiment suggests a precise conjecture, which is then confirmed
by a rigorous proof.

The paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:family} establishes a closed-form aspect ratio formula
(Theorem~\ref{thm:family}) for matrix IFS $\{f_k(\mathbf{v}) = AR_k(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{t}_k)\}$
with Jordan-basis isometries, extends it to IFS with mixed rotations and
reflections (Theorem~\ref{thm:mixed}), derives the scalar case as
Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}, and classifies the quadratic field of the aspect
ratio (Corollary~\ref{cor:field}).
Section~\ref{sec:family-N} applies the formula to a two-parameter family of
plane-filling tiles parameterised by $x^2-\ell x+N$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:family-N}).
Section~\ref{sec:metallic} specialises to tiles over~$\mathbb{Z}[i]$,
proves that every metallic ratio $\mu_m$ arises as
the reciprocal aspect ratio of such a tile
(Theorem~\ref{thm:metallic}), and shows that $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is the unique imaginary
quadratic ring where collinear tiles achieve metallic-ratio aspect ratios
(Corollary~\ref{cor:unique-Zi}).
Section~\ref{sec:td} applies the formula to the Twin Dragon
and proves the main result.

\section{The aspect ratio formula}\label{sec:family}

An \emph{iterated function system} (IFS) is a finite collection
of contractions $f_1,\ldots,f_N$ on a complete metric space.
By Hutchinson's theorem \cite{Hutchinson1981} there exists a unique
non-empty compact set, the \emph{attractor}, satisfying
$\mathcal{A} = f_1(\mathcal{A}) \cup \cdots \cup f_N(\mathcal{A})$.

Consider an IFS
\[
  f_k(\mathbf{v}) = A R_k(\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{t}_k), \qquad k=1,\ldots,N,
\]
where $A$ is a $2\times 2$ real matrix with complex eigenvalues
$\lambda,\bar\lambda\in\mathbb{C}\setminus\mathbb{R}$, $|\lambda|<1$,
and each $R_k$ is a real $2\times 2$ matrix that is an isometry
\emph{in the Jordan basis of $A$}: that is, $P^{-1}R_kP$ acts as
multiplication by some $\varepsilon_k\in\mathbb{C}$, $|\varepsilon_k|=1$,
where $P$ is the real matrix whose columns are $\mathrm{Re}(\mathbf{p})$
and $\mathrm{Im}(\mathbf{p})$ for an eigenvector $\mathbf{p}$ of $A$
for eigenvalue $\lambda$.
In the Jordan basis, writing $t_k' = (P^{-1}\mathbf{t}_k)^{(1)}
+ i(P^{-1}\mathbf{t}_k)^{(2)} \in \mathbb{C}$,
the centering condition is $\sum_{k=1}^N \varepsilon_k t_k' = 0$.

Define the \emph{digit variance} $\sigma^2 := \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_k|t_k'|^2 > 0$,
the \emph{weighted algebraic variance}
$\tau_\varepsilon := \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_k \varepsilon_k^2(t_k')^2 \in \mathbb{C}$,
the \emph{weight second moment} $\alpha_2 := \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_k \varepsilon_k^2 \in \mathbb{C}$,
and the \emph{weighted digit anisotropy}
\[
  \kappa_\varepsilon := \frac{|\tau_\varepsilon|}{\sigma^2} \in [0,1].
\]

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:kappa-invariant}
Under the Jordan basis change $\mathbf{t}_k \mapsto t_k' = P^{-1}\mathbf{t}_k$
(identified as a complex number), the digit anisotropy is preserved:
if we define $\kappa$ using the original digits $\mathbf{t}_k$ identified
as complex numbers $t_k$ via the standard basis, and $\kappa_\varepsilon$ using
the Jordan-basis digits $t_k'$, then when all $\varepsilon_k = 1$,
\[
  \kappa'
  := \frac{|\sum_k (t_k')^2|}{\sum_k |t_k'|^2}
   = \kappa
  := \frac{|\sum_k t_k^2|}{\sum_k |t_k|^2}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The columns of $P$ are $\mathrm{Re}(\mathbf{p})$ and $\mathrm{Im}(\mathbf{p})$,
so $P^{-1}$ acts on $\mathbb{R}^2\cong\mathbb{C}$ as multiplication by
$1/p$, where $p\in\mathbb{C}$ is the eigenvector regarded as a complex number.
Therefore $t_k' = t_k/p$, and
\[
  \kappa'
  = \frac{|\sum_k (t_k/p)^2|}{\sum_k |t_k/p|^2}
  = \frac{|\sum_k t_k^2|/|p|^2}{\sum_k |t_k|^2/|p|^2}
  = \kappa. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:family}
Let $A$ be a $2\times 2$ real matrix with eigenvalues
$\lambda,\bar\lambda\in\mathbb{C}\setminus\mathbb{R}$, $|\lambda|<1$,
and let $\{f_k(\mathbf{v})=AR_k(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{t}_k)\}_{k=1}^N$
be an equal-weight IFS where each $R_k$ is an isometry in the Jordan
basis of $A$ with corresponding complex factor $\varepsilon_k$,
$|\varepsilon_k|=1$.
Suppose the centering condition $\sum_k \varepsilon_k t_k' = 0$ holds
in the Jordan basis and $\sigma^2>0$.
Then the invariant measure has aspect ratio
\begin{equation}\label{eq:family}
  \mathrm{AR}^2 = \frac{|1-\lambda^2\alpha_2| - (1-|\lambda|^2)\,\kappa_\varepsilon}
                      {|1-\lambda^2\alpha_2| + (1-|\lambda|^2)\,\kappa_\varepsilon},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_2 = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_k\varepsilon_k^2$,
$\tau_\varepsilon = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_k\varepsilon_k^2(t_k')^2$,
and $\kappa_\varepsilon = |\tau_\varepsilon|/\sigma^2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:kappa-invariant} it suffices to work in the Jordan
basis of $A$, where $f_k(z) = \lambda\varepsilon_k(z + t_k')$
with $t_k'\in\mathbb{C}$.
Let $Z$ be distributed according to the invariant measure $\mu$.
The fixed-point equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fp}
  E[h(Z)] = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^N E\bigl[h\bigl(\lambda\varepsilon_k(Z + t_k')\bigr)\bigr]
\end{equation}
applied to $h(z)=z$ gives, using $\sum_k\varepsilon_k t_k'=0$,
\[
  E[Z] = \lambda\alpha_1 E[Z],
  \qquad \alpha_1 := \tfrac{1}{N}\textstyle\sum_k\varepsilon_k,
\]
so $E[Z]=0$ (since $|\lambda\alpha_1|\leq|\lambda|<1$).
Applying~\eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z)=z^2$:
\[
  E[Z^2] = \lambda^2\alpha_2\,E[Z^2] + \lambda^2\tau_\varepsilon,
  \qquad\text{so}\qquad
  E[Z^2] = \frac{\lambda^2\tau_\varepsilon}{1-\lambda^2\alpha_2}.
\]
Applying~\eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z)=|z|^2$, noting $|\varepsilon_k|=1$:
\[
  E[|Z|^2] = |\lambda|^2 E[|Z|^2] + |\lambda|^2\sigma^2,
  \qquad\text{so}\qquad
  E[|Z|^2] = \frac{|\lambda|^2\sigma^2}{1-|\lambda|^2}.
\]
The ellipticity ratio is
\[
  \rho = \frac{|E[Z^2]|}{E[|Z|^2]}
       = \frac{(1-|\lambda|^2)\,\kappa_\varepsilon}{|1-\lambda^2\alpha_2|}.
\]
The standard identity $\mathrm{AR}^2=(1-\rho)/(1+\rho)$
yields~\eqref{eq:family}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:mixed}
Let $A$, $\lambda$, and the Jordan basis be as in Theorem~\ref{thm:family}.
Partition the index set $\{1,\ldots,N\}$ into two classes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $I^+$: maps with \emph{rotations} in the Jordan basis,
    $f_k(z) = \lambda\varepsilon_k(z + t_k')$;
  \item $I^-$: maps with \emph{reflections} in the Jordan basis,
    $f_k(z) = \lambda\varepsilon_k(\bar{z} + t_k')$.
\end{itemize}
Define
\[
  \alpha_2^+ := \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k\in I^+}\varepsilon_k^2, \qquad
  \alpha_2^- := \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k\in I^-}\varepsilon_k^2,
\]
and suppose the generalised centering condition
\[
  \sum_{k\in I^+}\varepsilon_k t_k' + \sum_{k\in I^-}\varepsilon_k\overline{t_k'} = 0
\]
holds (when $I^-=\emptyset$ this reduces to the standard condition
$\sum_k\varepsilon_k t_k'=0$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:family}) and $\sigma^2 > 0$.  Set
\[
  \tau := \frac{1}{N}\!\left(\sum_{k\in I^+}\varepsilon_k^2(t_k')^2
                            + \sum_{k\in I^-}\varepsilon_k^2(\overline{t_k'})^2\right),
\]
and let
\[
  \Delta := \bigl(1-\lambda^2\alpha_2^+\bigr)\bigl(1-\bar\lambda^2\bar\alpha_2^+\bigr)
           - |\lambda|^4|\alpha_2^-|^2.
\]
If\/ $\Delta\neq 0$, the invariant measure has aspect ratio
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mixed}
  \mathrm{AR}^2 = \frac{1-\rho}{1+\rho},
  \qquad
  \rho = \frac{|E[Z^2]|}{E[|Z|^2]},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mixed-moments}
  E[Z^2] = \frac{\lambda^2\tau\,(1-\bar\lambda^2\bar\alpha_2^+)
                 + \lambda^2\alpha_2^-\bar\lambda^2\bar\tau}{\Delta},
  \qquad
  E[|Z|^2] = \frac{|\lambda|^2\sigma^2}{1-|\lambda|^2}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Work in the Jordan basis throughout.
Because $|\varepsilon_k|=1$ and $|\bar{z}|=|z|$, applying the
fixed-point equation~\eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z)=|z|^2$ gives
\[
  E[|Z|^2] = |\lambda|^2 E[|Z|^2] + |\lambda|^2\sigma^2,
\]
exactly as in Theorem~\ref{thm:family}, so $E[|Z|^2] = |\lambda|^2\sigma^2/(1-|\lambda|^2)$.

For $E[Z]$: applying~\eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z)=z$ and using the
generalised centering condition gives $E[Z]=0$.

Set $w := E[Z^2]$.  Applying~\eqref{eq:fp} to $h(z)=z^2$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a rotation map $f_k(z)=\lambda\varepsilon_k(z+t_k')$ contributes
    $\lambda^2\varepsilon_k^2\,E[(Z+t_k')^2]$;
  \item a reflection map $f_k(z)=\lambda\varepsilon_k(\bar{z}+t_k')$ contributes
    $\lambda^2\varepsilon_k^2\,E[(\bar{Z}+\overline{t_k'})^2]
     = \lambda^2\varepsilon_k^2(\bar{w}+(\overline{t_k'})^2)$.
\end{itemize}
Collecting terms (using $E[Z]=0$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mixed-w}
  w = \lambda^2\alpha_2^+\,w + \lambda^2\alpha_2^-\,\bar{w} + \lambda^2\tau.
\end{equation}
Taking complex conjugates of~\eqref{eq:mixed-w}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mixed-wbar}
  \bar{w} = \bar\lambda^2\bar\alpha_2^+\,\bar{w}
            + \bar\lambda^2\bar\alpha_2^-\,w + \bar\lambda^2\bar\tau.
\end{equation}
The system~\eqref{eq:mixed-w}--\eqref{eq:mixed-wbar} in unknowns $(w,\bar{w})$
has coefficient matrix
\[
  \begin{pmatrix}
    1-\lambda^2\alpha_2^+ & -\lambda^2\alpha_2^- \\
    -\bar\lambda^2\bar\alpha_2^- & 1-\bar\lambda^2\bar\alpha_2^+
  \end{pmatrix},
\]
with determinant $\Delta$.  Solving by Cramer's rule yields~\eqref{eq:mixed-moments},
and inserting into~\eqref{eq:mixed} gives the formula for~$\mathrm{AR}^2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:family} is the special case $I^-=\emptyset$ of
Theorem~\ref{thm:mixed}: when no reflections are present,
$\alpha_2^-=0$ and $\Delta=|1-\lambda^2\alpha_2^+|^2$,
so $E[Z^2]=\lambda^2\tau_\varepsilon/(1-\lambda^2\alpha_2^+)$,
recovering~\eqref{eq:family}.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:general}
Let $n \geq 1$ and consider the IFS in $\mathbb{R}^n$
\[
  f_k(\mathbf{v}) = A O_k (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{t}_k), \quad k = 1, \ldots, N,
\]
with equal weights $1/N$, where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $A$ is a real $n\times n$ contracting matrix (all eigenvalues satisfy
    $|\mu_i| < 1$);
  \item $O_k \in O(n)$ are arbitrary orthogonal matrices ($\det O_k = \pm 1$);
  \item $\mathbf{t}_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ are arbitrary translation vectors.
\end{itemize}
Suppose the centering condition
\[
  \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N A O_k \mathbf{t}_k = \mathbf{0}
\]
holds (i.e.\ the mean fixed point is at the origin).  Let $\Sigma = E[\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{Z}^\top]$
be the covariance matrix of the invariant measure (which satisfies $E[\mathbf{Z}]=\mathbf{0}$
by the centering condition).  Then $\Sigma$ is the unique solution of the
Lyapunov-type equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lyapunov}
  \Sigma = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N A O_k \!\left(\Sigma + \mathbf{t}_k \mathbf{t}_k^\top\right) O_k^\top A^\top.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, setting
\[
  M := \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N (AO_k) \otimes (AO_k),
  \qquad
  T := \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N A O_k \mathbf{t}_k \mathbf{t}_k^\top O_k^\top A^\top,
\]
equation~\eqref{eq:lyapunov} reads $(I - M)\,\operatorname{vec}(\Sigma) = \operatorname{vec}(T)$,
and $(I-M)$ is invertible because $\rho(M) < 1$.
The attractor of the IFS is an ellipsoid whose semi-axes are proportional to
$\sqrt{\lambda_1(\Sigma)} \geq \cdots \geq \sqrt{\lambda_n(\Sigma)}$,
where $\lambda_1(\Sigma) \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n(\Sigma)$ are the eigenvalues of $\Sigma$.
In particular, the aspect ratio is
\[
  \mathrm{AR} = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda_n(\Sigma)}{\lambda_1(\Sigma)}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The invariant measure $\mu$ of the IFS satisfies
$\mu = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^N \mu \circ f_k^{-1}$.
The centering condition ensures $E[\mathbf{Z}] = \mathbf{0}$, so
$\Sigma = E[\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{Z}^\top]$.  Applying the moment equation
$E[h(\mathbf{Z})] = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^N E[h(f_k(\mathbf{Z}))]$
to $h(\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^\top$ gives
\begin{align*}
  \Sigma
  &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^N E\!\left[\bigl(AO_k(\mathbf{Z}+\mathbf{t}_k)\bigr)
                                       \bigl(AO_k(\mathbf{Z}+\mathbf{t}_k)\bigr)^\top\right] \\
  &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^N AO_k\,E\!\left[(\mathbf{Z}+\mathbf{t}_k)(\mathbf{Z}+\mathbf{t}_k)^\top\right]
     O_k^\top A^\top \\
  &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^N AO_k\!\left(\Sigma + \mathbf{t}_k\mathbf{t}_k^\top\right)O_k^\top A^\top,
\end{align*}
which is~\eqref{eq:lyapunov} (using $E[\mathbf{Z}]=\mathbf{0}$).
Vectorising via $\operatorname{vec}(PQR) = (R^\top\otimes P)\operatorname{vec}(Q)$ yields
$(I-M)\operatorname{vec}(\Sigma) = \operatorname{vec}(T)$.
Since all eigenvalues of $AO_k$ satisfy $|\mu_i| \leq \|AO_k\|$, and $A$ is contracting,
the eigenvalues of $M$ satisfy $|\nu_j(M)| \leq \rho(A)^2 < 1$, so $\rho(M)<1$,
$(I-M)$ is invertible, and $\Sigma$ is uniquely determined.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The maps $f_k(x) = s_k g^{-1}(x) + y_k$ of \cite{Bandt1991}, where $g$ is a
linear expansive map with integer matrix and $s_k$ are elements of a symmetry
group of $g$, are a special case of Theorem~\ref{thm:general} with $A = g^{-1}$
and $O_k = s_k$ (the affine part of $s_k$ is absorbed into the translation
$\mathbf{t}_k$).  In particular, all examples in \cite{Bandt1991} involving
rotations and reflections fall under Theorem~\ref{thm:general}.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:scalar}
When $A = aI$ for $a\in\mathbb{C}$, $|a|<1$, and all $R_k = I$
(so $\varepsilon_k=1$ and $\alpha_2=1$), the Jordan basis coincides
with the standard basis, $\lambda=a$, $\kappa_\varepsilon = \kappa$,
and~\eqref{eq:family} reduces to
\[
  \mathrm{AR}^2 = \frac{|1-a^2| - (1-|a|^2)\,\kappa}
                      {|1-a^2| + (1-|a|^2)\,\kappa},
\]
which is the aspect ratio formula for the scalar IFS
$\{f_k(z)=a(z+t_k)\}$ over $\mathbb{C}$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:special-cases}
Theorem~\ref{thm:family} unifies several previously separate results.
The following table summarises the main special cases.

\medskip
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccp{4.5cm}}
\hline
$A$ & $R_k$ & $\varepsilon_k$ & Result \\
\hline
$aI$ & $I$ & $1$ & scalar formula (Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}) \\
$aI$ & $\pm I$ & $\pm 1$ & sign-flip (Remark~\ref{rem:sign-flip}) \\
$aI$ & rotations & $e^{i\theta_k}$ & rotation-weights case \\
general $A$ & Jordan-basis isometries & $|\varepsilon_k|=1$ & Theorem~\ref{thm:family} \\
general $A$ & rotations + reflections & mixed $\varepsilon_k$ & Theorem~\ref{thm:mixed} \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip

Three further special cases of the general formula are noteworthy:
\begin{enumerate}[(a)]
\item \textbf{Sign flips} ($\varepsilon_k=\pm 1$): then $\alpha_2=1$ and
  $\kappa_\varepsilon = |\sum_k \varepsilon_k^2(t_k')^2|/\sigma^2$,
  recovering Remark~\ref{rem:sign-flip}.
\item \textbf{Uniform rotation} ($\varepsilon_k = e^{i\theta}$ for all $k$):
  then $\alpha_2 = e^{2i\theta}$ and $\tau_\varepsilon = e^{2i\theta}\tau$,
  so $\kappa_\varepsilon = \kappa$ and the AR is unchanged â€” the attractor
  is merely rotated by $\theta$.
\item \textbf{$N$-th roots of unity} ($\varepsilon_k = e^{2\pi i k/N}$,
  $N\geq 3$): then $\alpha_2 = 0$
  and~\eqref{eq:family} reduces to
  $\mathrm{AR}^2 = (1-(1-|\lambda|^2)\kappa_\varepsilon)/(1+(1-|\lambda|^2)\kappa_\varepsilon)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}
\begin{remark}\label{rem:sign-flip}
This remark details case~(a) of Remark~\ref{rem:special-cases}.
This is a special case of Theorem~\ref{thm:family} with $\varepsilon_k = \pm 1$.
The formula extends to sign-flip IFS
$f_k(z) = \varepsilon_k a(z + t_k)$ with
$\varepsilon_k \in \{+1,-1\}$.
The centering condition becomes
$\sum \varepsilon_k t_k = 0$;
when it holds, $\varepsilon_k^2 = 1$ ensures that $E[Z^2]$ and
$E[|Z|^2]$ reduce to the same expressions as in
Theorem~\ref{thm:family}, so~\eqref{eq:family} applies as stated.
When the IFS is not centred
($\sum\varepsilon_k t_k\neq 0$), the formula still holds with
$\kappa$ replaced by
\[
  \tilde\kappa
  = \frac{|\tau - \beta^2(1-a^2)|}{\sigma^2 - |\beta|^2(1-|a|^2)},
  \qquad
  \beta = \frac{\textstyle\sum\varepsilon_k t_k}
               {N - a\!\sum\varepsilon_k}.
\]
For $N=2$ every sign pattern gives $\tilde\kappa = \kappa$
(signs are invisible); for $N \geq 3$ they enlarge the range of
achievable aspect ratios.
\end{remark}

When the contraction ratio is the reciprocal of a Gaussian integer
and the translations are collinear ($\kappa=1$), the
aspect ratio is a quadratic irrational whose field is determined by a
single Gaussian norm.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:field}
Let $\lambda \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$ with $|\lambda|>1$ and $\lambda\notin\mathbb{R}$,
and set $a = 1/\lambda$.  For any IFS with $\kappa=1$,
the aspect ratio~\eqref{eq:family} lies
in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d}\,)$,
where $d$ is the square-free part of $\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)$.
Explicitly,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:field}
  \mathrm{AR}^2
  = \frac{\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)} - (\mathbf{N}(\lambda)-1)}
         {\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)} + (\mathbf{N}(\lambda)-1)}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Substitute $|a|^2 = 1/\mathbf{N}(\lambda)$,
$|1-a^2| = \sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)}/\mathbf{N}(\lambda)$,
and $\kappa=1$
into the scalar formula of Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}.
\end{proof}

because the rational prime~$5$ divides both norms.
This is not a coincidence: the Cayley-type transform~\eqref{eq:field}
maps $\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)}$ to an element of the
real quadratic field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d}\,)$, and the particular
metallic ratio that appears is controlled by the arithmetic of this field.
In the first row, $\sqrt{5}$ yields a rational expression in $\varphi$
thanks to the identity $\varphi^2 = \varphi+1$: the golden ratio is the
fundamental unit of the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\varphi]$, so every element of
$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$ in $(0,1)$ of the form
$(\sqrt{5}-n)/(\sqrt{5}+n)$ is a power of $1/\varphi$.
The third row gives the \emph{silver ratio} $\delta_S = \sqrt{2}-1$
(fundamental unit of $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]$).

\smallskip
Corollary~\ref{cor:field} assumes $\kappa=1$.
The following extension covers arbitrary~$\kappa$.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:field-gen}
For any IFS $\{f_k(z)=a(z+t_k)\}$ with $a=1/\lambda$,
$\lambda\in\mathbb{Z}[i]\setminus\mathbb{R}$,
and digits $t_k\in\mathbb{Z}[i]$,
the anisotropy satisfies $\kappa^2\in\mathbb{Q}$,
and $\mathrm{AR}^2$ lies in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d}\,)$
where $d$ is the square-free part of
$\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)\cdot\bigl(\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)
  - \kappa^2(\mathbf{N}(\lambda)-1)^2\bigr)$.
In particular, the quadratic field of $\mathrm{AR}$ depends only on
$\lambda$ and~$\kappa^2$, not on the individual digits.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Write $\tau = \sum(t_k-\bar t)^2 \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$
and $\sigma^2 = \sum |t_k-\bar t|^2 \in \mathbb{Z}$.
Then $\kappa^2 = |\tau|^2/\sigma^4 = \mathbf{N}(\tau)/\sigma^4$,
which is rational since $\mathbf{N}(\tau)\in\mathbb{Z}$.
Substituting into the scalar formula (Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}) gives
\[
  \mathrm{AR}^2
  = \frac{|1-a^2| - (1-|a|^2)\kappa}{|1-a^2| + (1-|a|^2)\kappa}
  = \frac{\sqrt{R} - (N\!-\!1)\kappa}
         {\sqrt{R} + (N\!-\!1)\kappa},
\]
where $R = \mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)/N^2$.
(here $N = |\lambda|^2 = \mathbf{N}(\lambda)$ equals both the norm and the number of maps).
Clearing $N^2$: the numerator and denominator are
$\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)} \mp (N\!-\!1)\kappa$.
Rationalising, $\mathrm{AR}^2 = 1 - 2(N\!-\!1)\kappa/
\bigl(\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)}+(N\!-\!1)\kappa\bigr)$,
and since $(N\!-\!1)\kappa$ is
$\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\tau)}/\sigma^2 \cdot (N\!-\!1)/N$
--- a rational multiple of $\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\tau)}$,
while $\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)}$ is
algebraic of degree~$\leq 2$,
$\mathrm{AR}^2$ lies in a quadratic extension of~$\mathbb{Q}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
For collinear translations ($\kappa=1$),
formula~\eqref{eq:family} shows that $\mathrm{AR}$ depends on $a$ only
through $(|a|^2, |1-a^2|)$: the attractor is nearly round when $|1-a^2|$
is large relative to $1-|a|^2$, and degenerates to a segment when
$|1-a^2|\approx 1-|a|^2$ (i.e.\ $a$ nearly real).
For every $a$ in the family, $\mathrm{AR}(a) \leq |a|$, with equality
if and only if $a$ is purely imaginary.
Indeed, $|1-a^2| \leq 1+|a|^2$ (triangle inequality), with equality iff
$a^2/|a^2|=-1$, i.e.\ $a\in i\mathbb{R}$.
Substituting $|1-a^2|=1+|a|^2$ into~\eqref{eq:family} gives
$\mathrm{AR}^2 = |a|^2$.
Figure~\ref{fig:ar} shows $\mathrm{AR}$ over the entire sub-family $|a|=1/\sqrt{2}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:orientation}
In the scalar case of Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar} (all $\varepsilon_k=1$, $A=aI$),
the covariance matrix also determines the \emph{orientation} of the
principal axes.  Writing $E[Z^2] = \rho e^{2i\psi}$ ($\rho > 0$) for
$Z = X + iY$ with $E[Z]=0$, the matrix decomposes as
\[
  M = \frac{E[|Z|^2]}{2}\,I
    + \frac{\rho}{2}
      \begin{pmatrix}\cos 2\psi & \sin 2\psi \\ \sin 2\psi & -\cos 2\psi\end{pmatrix},
\]
whose larger eigenvalue has eigenvector $(\cos\psi, \sin\psi)$.
Hence the major axis of the attractor makes angle
\[
  \psi = \tfrac{1}{2}\arg\!\left(\tfrac{a^2\tau}{1-a^2}\right)
       = \tfrac{1}{2}\arg\!\left(\tfrac{a^2}{1-a^2}\right)
         + \tfrac{1}{2}\arg(\tau)
\]
with the positive $x$-axis.
The first term depends only on the contraction~$a$;
the second rotates the principal axes by half the argument of the
algebraic variance $\tau = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum t_k^2$.
For collinear translations $\tau > 0$ and the second term vanishes.
\end{remark}

\section{The \texorpdfstring{$\lambda_{N,\ell}$}{Î»\_{N,â„“}} family}\label{sec:family-N}

A natural two-parameter family of plane-filling tiles arises
by applying Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar} to collinear digits.
For integers $N\geq 2$ and $0 \leq \ell < 2\sqrt{N}$, let
$\lambda_{N,\ell}$ be a root of $x^2-\ell x+N$
(so $|\lambda_{N,\ell}|^2=N$ and $\lambda_{N,\ell}\notin\mathbb{R}$).
The digits $\{0,1,\ldots,N\!-\!1\}$ form a complete residue system (CRS) of
$\mathbb{Z}[\lambda_{N,\ell}]$ modulo~$\lambda_{N,\ell}$.
Indeed, every element $a+b\lambda_{N,\ell}\in\mathbb{Z}[\lambda_{N,\ell}]$
satisfies $\lambda_{N,\ell}(a+b\lambda_{N,\ell})
= a\lambda_{N,\ell}+b(\ell\lambda_{N,\ell}-N)$
(using $\lambda_{N,\ell}^2=\ell\lambda_{N,\ell}-N$),
whose integer part is $-bN$;
hence $a+b\lambda_{N,\ell}\equiv 0\pmod{\lambda_{N,\ell}}$ forces
$b=0$ and $N\mid a$, so
$\lambda_{N,\ell}\mathbb{Z}[\lambda_{N,\ell}]\cap\mathbb{Z}=N\mathbb{Z}$
and the $N$ residues $\{0,\ldots,N\!-\!1\}$ are distinct.
The $N$-map IFS with $a=1/\lambda_{N,\ell}$ and digits centred
to $\sum t_j=0$ therefore produces a tile.
The aspect ratio and orientation of these tiles
admit explicit formulas.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:family-N}
Let $N\geq 2$ and $0 \leq \ell < 2\sqrt{N}$, and let $\lambda = \lambda_{N,\ell}$
be a root of $x^2-\ell x+N$.
For the $N$-map tiling IFS with contraction $a = 1/\lambda$:
\begin{enumerate}[{\upshape (i)}]
\item $|\lambda^2-1|^2 = (N+1)^2 - \ell^2$, and the aspect ratio is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:family-N}
  \mathrm{AR}_{N,\ell}
  = \sqrt{\frac{\sqrt{(N+1)^2-\ell^2}\;-(N-1)}
               {\sqrt{(N+1)^2-\ell^2}\;+(N-1)}}\,.
\end{equation}
\item The major axis of inertia makes angle
\begin{equation}\label{eq:psi-N}
  \psi_{N,\ell}
  = -\tfrac{1}{2}\arg(\lambda^2-1)
\end{equation}
with the positive $x$-axis, where
$\lambda^2-1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl((\ell^2\!-\!2N\!-\!2) + i\ell\sqrt{4N-\ell^2}\bigr)$.
\item When $\ell=2$ \textup{(}the sub-family
$\lambda_N = 1+i\sqrt{N\!-\!1}$\textup{)},
\eqref{eq:family-N} simplifies to
$\mathrm{AR}_N = (\sqrt{N+3}-\sqrt{N-1})/2$,
and $\tan\psi$ satisfies $t^2+t\sqrt{N\!-\!1}-1=0$.
When additionally $N\!-\!1=m^2$, this is the
$m$-th metallic-ratio equation:
$m=1$ gives $\varphi$, $m=2$ gives $1+\sqrt{2}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Part~(i): Expanding $\lambda = (\ell+i\sqrt{4N-\ell^2})/2$ gives
\[
  |\lambda^2-1|^2
  = \tfrac{1}{4}\bigl[(\ell^2\!-\!2N\!-\!2)^2 + \ell^2(4N-\ell^2)\bigr]
  = (N+1)^2 - \ell^2.
\]
Substituting $|a|^2 = 1/N$ and $|1-a^2| = \sqrt{(N+1)^2-\ell^2}/N$
into the scalar formula~\eqref{eq:family} (Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}) yields~\eqref{eq:family-N}.
Part~(ii) follows from $a^2/(1-a^2) = 1/(\lambda^2-1)$
and Remark~\ref{rem:orientation}.
Part~(iii): Setting $\ell=2$ gives
$(N+1)^2-4 = (N-1)(N+3)$; the AR formula rationalises to
$(\sqrt{N+3}-\sqrt{N-1})/2$.
For the tangent, $\lambda^2-1 = (1-N) + 2i\sqrt{N\!-\!1}$;
the double-angle identity $\tan 2\psi = 2\tan\psi/(1-\tan^2\!\psi)
= 2/\sqrt{N\!-\!1}$ yields the stated equation.
\end{proof}

generally a unit.

\begin{remark}
Both the aspect ratio and the principal-axis orientation of the
$\lambda_{N,\ell}$-family are governed by a single element of the
ring of integers: $\lambda^2-1 = \ell\lambda-(N\!+\!1) \in \mathbb{Z}[\lambda]$.

via~\eqref{eq:family-N}, and its argument determines~$\psi$
via~\eqref{eq:psi-N}.
This admits a hierarchical interpretation.
The random-iteration representation
$Z = \sum_{n\geq 1} a^n\varepsilon_n$
\textup{(}$\varepsilon_n$ i.i.d.\ from the centred digits\textup{)}
decomposes $E[Z^2]$ as $\sum_{n\geq 1}\sigma^2 a^{2n}$:
each level~$n$ of the IFS contributes $\sigma^2/N^n$ to the
anisotropy along direction $2n\arg(a) = -2n\arg(\lambda)$.
The geometric sum of these rotated contributions points in the
direction $\arg\bigl(a^2/(1-a^2)\bigr) = -\arg(\lambda^2-1)$,
which is precisely twice the principal-axis angle.
At the boundary $\ell=0$ the vector $\lambda^2-1 = -(N\!+\!1)$ is
real negative, giving $\psi = -\pi/2$ (vertical major axis);
as $\ell\to 2\sqrt{N}$ the vector tends to $2(N\!-\!1)>0$,
giving $\psi\to 0$ (horizontal, degenerate).
\end{remark}

\section{Tiles over \texorpdfstring{$\mathbb{Z}[i]$}{â„¤[i]}}\label{sec:metallic}

In this section we specialise to tiles
over~$\mathbb{Z}[i]$: the contraction is $a = 1/\lambda$ for
a Gaussian integer~$\lambda$ and the translations form a
CRS modulo~$\lambda$.
Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar} then applies with $|a|^2=1/N$ and
$\kappa$ determined by the choice of CRS.

The dependence on $\kappa$ is already visible for a single
expansion factor.
Take $\lambda = 2+i$ ($N\!=\!5$).
The collinear digit set $\{0,1,2,3,4\}$ gives $\kappa=1$ and
$\mathrm{AR}=1/\varphi^3$ (row~$(5,4)$ of the table in
Section~\ref{sec:family-N}).
Replacing it by the two-dimensional CRS
$\{0,\,1,\,-i,\,i,\,1\!+\!i\}$
(which is still a complete residue system of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$
modulo~$2\!+\!i$, since $i\equiv 3\pmod{2\!+\!i}$)
yields $\sigma^2=4/5$, $\tau=(-8+6i)/25$, and $\kappa=1/2$.
Substituting into the scalar formula (Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar}):
\[
  \mathrm{AR}^2
  = \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{\sqrt{5}+1}
  = \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}
  = \frac{1}{\varphi^2},
  \qquad
  \mathrm{AR} = \frac{1}{\varphi}.
\]
Thus the same golden ratio governs this $5$-map tile with $\kappa=1/2$
as it does the Twin Dragon ($N\!=\!2$, $\kappa\!=\!1$),
while collinear digits for the same~$\lambda$ give the smaller
value~$1/\varphi^3$ (Figure~\ref{fig:kappa-tile}).
A natural question arises: for which~$\kappa$ does the aspect ratio
equal $1/\mu_m$, the reciprocal of the $m$-th metallic mean?

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:golden}
Let $\lambda = p+qi \in \mathbb{Z}[i]\setminus\mathbb{R}$ with
$|\lambda|^2 = N$, and let $m\geq 1$ be a positive integer.
The digit anisotropy required for $\mathrm{AR}=1/\mu_m$
\textup{(}the reciprocal $m$-th metallic mean
$\mu_m=(m+\sqrt{m^2+4})/2$\textup{)} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kappa-m}
  \kappa_m^*
  = \frac{m\,|\lambda^2-1|}{\sqrt{m^2+4}\,(N-1)},
\end{equation}
and $\kappa_m^*\leq 1$ if and only if $mq\leq N-1$.
In particular:
\begin{enumerate}[\upshape(a)]
\item For $m=1$ \textup{(}golden ratio\textup{)},
  $\kappa_1^*\leq 1$ for every~$\lambda$,
  with equality only for $\lambda=1+i$.
\item The collinear case $\kappa=1$ corresponds to
  $m=(N\!-\!1)/q$, provided this is an integer;
  thus the collinear aspect ratio of
  Corollary~\textup{\ref{cor:field}} equals
  $1/\mu_{(N-1)/q}$.
\item For $q=1$ \textup{(}i.e.\ $\lambda=p+i$\textup{)},
  every metallic ratio $\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_{p^2}$ is accessible.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Setting $\mathrm{AR}^2 = 1/\mu_m^2$ in the scalar formula~\eqref{eq:family} (Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar})
and solving for $\kappa$ gives
$\kappa = |1-a^2|\cdot m/\bigl((1-|a|^2)\sqrt{m^2+4}\bigr)$;
substituting $a=1/\lambda$, $|a|^2=1/N$,
$|1-a^2|=\sqrt{\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)}/N$
yields~\eqref{eq:kappa-m}.

The condition $\kappa_m^*\leq 1$ becomes
$m^2|\lambda^2-1|^2 \leq (m^2+4)(N\!-\!1)^2$.
Since $|\lambda^2-1|^2 = (N\!-\!1)^2+4q^2$, this simplifies to
$4m^2q^2 \leq 4(N\!-\!1)^2$, i.e.\ $mq\leq N\!-\!1$.

\textit{(a)}: $q\leq N-1$ holds since $N=p^2+q^2\geq q+1$ for $p\geq 1$.
Equality $q=N\!-\!1$ gives $p^2=1-q(q\!-\!1)$;
for $q=1$ this yields $p=1$, $\lambda=1+i$;
for $q\geq 2$ one has $p^2<0$, which is impossible.

\textit{(b)}: $\kappa_m^*=1$ iff $m^2q^2=(N\!-\!1)^2$,
so $m=(N\!-\!1)/q$.

\textit{(c)}: $q=1$ gives $mq = m \leq N-1 = p^2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:half}
Up to associates, $\kappa_1^*=1/2$ if and only if
$\lambda\in\{2+i,\;2+3i,\;1+4i\}$ \textup{(}$N=5,13,17$\textup{)}.
More generally, $\kappa_1^* = 1/m$ for an integer $m\geq 1$ only for
$m=1$ \textup{(}$\lambda=1+i$\textup{)} and $m=2$
\textup{(}the three primes above\textup{)}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Write $\lambda=p+qi$, $N=p^2+q^2$.  Setting $\kappa_1^*=1/m$ in
$|\lambda^2-1|^2 = 5(N-1)^2/m^2$ and using
$|\lambda^2-1|^2 = (N-1)^2+4q^2$ gives
\[
  4q^2 = (N\!-\!1)^2\!\bigl(\tfrac{5}{m^2}-1\bigr).
\]
For $m=1$ this is $4q^2=4(N\!-\!1)^2$, so $q=N\!-\!1=1$, $N=2$:
only $\lambda=1+i$.
For $m=2$: $4q^2 = \frac14(N\!-\!1)^2$, so $N=4q+1$, $p^2=4q+1-q^2$,
and the positivity condition $p^2\geq 1$ becomes
$p^2+(q-2)^2 = 5$, i.e.\ a representation of~$5$ as a sum of two
squares.  The three solutions with $q\geq 1$, $p\geq 1$ are
$(p,q)=(2,1),(2,3),(1,4)$.
For $m\geq 3$: $5/m^2-1 < 0$, so $4q^2<0$ --- impossible.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:metallic}
For every integer $m\geq 1$:
\begin{enumerate}[\upshape(a)]
\item There exists a self-similar plane-filling tile
  in~$\mathbb{R}^2$ whose aspect ratio equals
  $1/\mu_m$, the reciprocal of the $m$-th metallic mean.
\item Any such tile over $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ requires at least
  $N_{\min}(m) = \lceil\sqrt{m}\,\rceil^2 + 1$ maps.
\item When $m$ is a perfect square, $N_{\min}(m) = m+1$, achieved
  by the collinear CRS for $\lambda = \sqrt{m}+i$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textit{(a):}
Let $\lambda = 1+mi \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$.
Then $N = |\lambda|^2 = m^2+1$ and $\operatorname{Im}(\lambda)=m$.
The standard digits $\{0,1,\ldots,m^2\}$ form a CRS
modulo~$\lambda$ (\S\ref{sec:family-N});
the induced $N$-map IFS satisfies the open set
condition~\cite{Bandt1991}, so its attractor is a
plane-filling tile.
The collinear digit set has $\kappa = 1$, and
Proposition~\ref{prop:golden}(b) gives
$m = (N\!-\!1)/\operatorname{Im}(\lambda) = m^2/m$,
hence $\mathrm{AR} = 1/\mu_m$.

\textit{(b):}
For any $\lambda=p+qi$ with $mq\leq N\!-\!1$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:golden}), we have
$N = p^2+q^2 \geq q(m\!-\!q)+1+q^2 = qm+1$,
which is minimised at~$q=1$, giving
$N \geq \lceil\sqrt{m}\,\rceil^2+1$.

\textit{(c):}
When $m=k^2$, the choice $\lambda=k+i$ yields $N=k^2+1=m+1$ and
the collinear CRS has $\kappa=1$ with $(N\!-\!1)/q=m$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Not every integer $N\geq 2$ is the norm of a Gaussian integer with
non-zero imaginary part.
By the sum-of-two-squares theorem, $N=p^2+q^2$ with $q\neq 0$
if and only if every prime factor of~$N$ congruent to~$3\pmod{4}$
appears to an even power.
The first admissible values are $N=2,4,5,8,9,10,13,\ldots$;
in particular $N=3,6,7,11,14,15$ are not representable.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
Item~(b) of Proposition~\ref{prop:golden} recovers every row of
Table~\ref{tab:examples} as a special case:
$\lambda=1\!+\!i$ gives $m=1$ and $\mathrm{AR}=1/\mu_1=1/\varphi$;
$\lambda=2\!+\!i$ gives $m=4$ and
$\mathrm{AR}=1/\mu_4=1/(2\!+\!\sqrt{5})=\sqrt{5}-2=1/\varphi^3$;
$\lambda=1\!+\!2i$ gives $m=2$ and $\mathrm{AR}=1/\mu_2=\sqrt{2}-1$.
Thus the collinear aspect ratios of Section~\ref{sec:family} and the

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:quadratic}
Let $\lambda\in\mathbb{C}\setminus\mathbb{R}$ with $|\lambda|^2=N\geq 2$.
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:imag-identity}
  |\lambda^2-1|^2 = (N-1)^2 + 4\,(\operatorname{Im}\lambda)^2,
\end{equation}
and the digit anisotropy~\eqref{eq:kappa-m} satisfies
$\kappa_m^*\leq 1$ if and only if
$m\leq (N\!-\!1)/|\operatorname{Im}\lambda|$.
The collinear case $\kappa=1$ corresponds to
$m=(N\!-\!1)/|\operatorname{Im}\lambda|$, which is a positive integer
only when $|\operatorname{Im}\lambda|$ divides $N\!-\!1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\lambda=x+iy$,  so $N=x^2+y^2$ and
$\lambda^2-1=(x^2\!-\!y^2\!-\!1)+2ixy$.  Then
\[
  |\lambda^2-1|^2
  = (x^2\!-\!y^2\!-\!1)^2 + 4x^2y^2
  = (x^2\!+\!y^2\!-\!1)^2 + 4y^2
  = (N\!-\!1)^2 + 4y^2,
\]
since, by direct expansion,
$(x^2\!-\!y^2\!-\!1)^2+4x^2y^2
  - (x^2\!+\!y^2\!-\!1)^2 = 4y^2$.
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:kappa-m}:
$\kappa_m^*\leq 1$
iff $m^2\bigl((N\!-\!1)^2+4y^2\bigr)\leq (m^2\!+\!4)(N\!-\!1)^2$,
i.e.\ $4m^2y^2\leq 4(N\!-\!1)^2$, so $m\leq (N\!-\!1)/|y|$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:unique-Zi}
Among all imaginary quadratic integer rings, $\mathbb{Z}[i]$
is the unique one where collinear self-similar tiles can have
metallic-ratio aspect ratios.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For $\lambda=p+q\sqrt{-k}\in\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-k}]$
($k\geq 2$, square-free, $q\neq 0$),
$\operatorname{Im}\lambda = q\sqrt{k}\notin\mathbb{Q}$,
so $(N\!-\!1)/\operatorname{Im}\lambda$ is irrational.
The same holds for the full ring of integers of
$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-3})$: if $\lambda=a+b\omega\in\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$
with $\omega=e^{2\pi i/3}$ and $b\neq 0$, then
$\operatorname{Im}\lambda = b\sqrt{3}/2\notin\mathbb{Q}$.
In $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, however,
$\operatorname{Im}\lambda = q\in\mathbb{Z}$,
and taking $q=1$ gives $m=(N\!-\!1)/1 = p^2$, always a positive integer.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:eisenstein}
Proposition~\ref{prop:quadratic} shows that the identity
$|\lambda^2\!-\!1|^2\!-\!(N\!-\!1)^2$ is controlled entirely by
$\operatorname{Im}\lambda$.
Specialising to each ring:
$\mathbb{Z}[i]$: $4q^2$;\;
$\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$: $3b^2$;\;
$\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-k}]$: $4kq^2$.
The upper bound $m_{\max}=(N\!-\!1)/|\operatorname{Im}\lambda|$
is rational only for~$\mathbb{Z}[i]$.
Nonetheless, by choosing $\kappa<1$ (non-collinear digits),
$\kappa_m^*\leq 1$ is guaranteed for every imaginary quadratic ring
whenever~$N$ is large enough.
A computational search over $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ confirms that
complete residue systems achieving $\kappa=\kappa_1^*$
(i.e.\ $\mathrm{AR}=1/\varphi$) exist for all Eisenstein primes
of norm $N\geq 7$.
However, for $N=3$ ($\lambda=1\!-\!\omega$, $\kappa_1^*=\sqrt{7/20}$),
an exhaustive search over shifts $|c_k|\leq 8$ fails to achieve
$\kappa_1^*$ (best approximation $\approx 3\times 10^{-5}$),
suggesting that three digits provide too few degrees of freedom
in the hexagonal lattice.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:ncol}
For \emph{collinear} digits ($\kappa=1$) the condition
$m=(N\!-\!1)/q$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:golden}(b)) requires
$p^2=q(m\!-\!q)+1$ to be a perfect square.
Taking $q=m$ gives the universal upper bound
$N_{\mathrm{col}}(m) \leq m^2+1$,
but smaller~$q$ may yield a smaller~$N$.
For $m=k^2$ a perfect square, $q=1$ gives
$N_{\mathrm{col}}(k^2) = k^2+1 = N_{\min}(k^2)$,
so the collinear and non-collinear minima coincide.
In contrast, for non-square~$m$ a gap opens:
e.g.\ $N_{\min}(5)=10$ while $N_{\mathrm{col}}(5)=26$,
and $N_{\min}(7)=10$ while $N_{\mathrm{col}}(7)=50$.
In general, $N_{\mathrm{col}}(m)$ equals the minimum of
$q^2 + q(m\!-\!q) + 1$ over those $q\in\{1,\ldots,m\}$ for which
$q(m\!-\!q)+1$ is a perfect square ---
a Diophantine condition whose behaviour mirrors the distribution of
square values of binary quadratic forms.
\end{remark}

\section{The Twin Dragon}\label{sec:td}

The Twin Dragon $A \subset \mathbb{C}$ is the attractor of the IFS \cite{DavisKnuth1970}
\begin{equation}
  A = f_1(A) \cup f_2(A),
  \qquad
  f_{1,2}(z) = \frac{1-i}{2}(z \pm 1).
\end{equation}
Both maps have the same contraction ratio $r = |{(1-i)/2}| = 1/\sqrt{2}$,
so the similarity dimension is $\alpha = -\log 2 / \log r = 2$.
The open set condition holds \cite[Corollary~3]{Bandt1991}: the Twin Dragon
is a digit tile for $\mathbb{Z}[i]/(1+i)\mathbb{Z}[i]$, so its
translates by $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ tile~$\mathbb{C}$ with the interiors
pairwise disjoint.  By \cite[\S5]{Hutchinson1981} the
self-similar measure therefore coincides with $\mathcal{H}^\alpha|_A$.
Since $\alpha=2$ equals the ambient dimension, $\mathcal{H}^2$ is Lebesgue
area, and the covariance matrix $M$ describes
the actual geometric shape (Figure~\ref{fig:td}).

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=6cm]{twin-dragon.png}
\caption{The Twin Dragon with its $1.5$-sigma principal-axis ellipse
(red).  The major axis lies along $(1,-\varphi)$ and the minor axis
along $(\varphi,1)$; their ratio of lengths equals the aspect ratio
$\mathrm{AR}=1/\varphi\approx 0.618$.}
\label{fig:td}
\end{figure}

Substituting $a = (1-i)/2$ into Corollary~\ref{cor:scalar} (the scalar case of Theorem~\ref{thm:family})
(with $N=2$, $t_k=\pm1$, so $\sigma^2=1$):
$|a|^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $a^2 = -\tfrac{i}{2}$,
$1-a^2 = \tfrac{2+i}{2}$, so
\[
  E[Z^2] = \frac{a^2}{1-a^2} = \frac{-i}{2+i} = \frac{-1-2i}{5},
  \qquad
  E[|Z|^2] = \frac{|a|^2}{1-|a|^2} = 1.
\]
The covariance matrix follows via
$M_{11}=\tfrac{1}{2}(E[|Z|^2]+\mathrm{Re}\,E[Z^2])$,
$M_{22}=\tfrac{1}{2}(E[|Z|^2]-\mathrm{Re}\,E[Z^2])$,
$M_{12}=\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Im}\,E[Z^2]$:
\begin{equation}
  M = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}2 & -1 \\ -1 & 3\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The matrix $M$ has $\operatorname{tr} M = 1$ and $\det M = \frac{1}{5}$,
so its eigenvalues are
\begin{equation}
  I_{1,2} = \frac{1 \mp \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{5}}}{2}.
\end{equation}
The geometric aspect ratio --- the ratio of standard deviations along
the principal axes of $M$ --- is
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{\frac{I_1}{I_2}}
  = \sqrt{\frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{\sqrt{5}+1}}
  = \sqrt{\frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}}
  = \frac{1}{\varphi},
\end{equation}
where $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.

\begin{corollary}
The attractor of the IFS $\bigl\{f_{1,2}(z) = \tfrac{1-i}{2}(z \pm 1)\bigr\}$
has covariance matrix $M = \tfrac{1}{5}\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}2&-1\\-1&3\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$,
geometric aspect ratio $1/\varphi = (\sqrt{5}-1)/2 \approx 0.618$,
and major-axis direction $(1, -\varphi)$
(angle $-\arctan\varphi \approx -58.3^\circ$ with the horizontal),
where $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
This is the case $\lambda = 1+i$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:field}
($\mathbf{N}(\lambda^2-1)=5$, $d=5$), with the orientation computed
from the general formula in Remark~\ref{rem:orientation}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The Twin Dragon is the $(N,\ell)=(2,2)$ instance of
Proposition~\ref{prop:family-N}, giving
$\tan\psi = -\varphi$ (i.e.\
$\psi \approx -58.3^\circ$).
Thus the same constant~$\varphi$ governs both the aspect ratio
($\mathrm{AR} = 1/\varphi$) and the orientation
($\tan\psi = -\varphi$).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The appearance of $\varphi$ is unexpected: the Twin Dragon is defined
by the Gaussian integer $1+i$, with no pentagon, decagon, or Fibonacci
recurrence in the construction.
The number $5$ --- and hence $\varphi$ --- can be traced to a single
Gaussian prime.  By the ergodic theorem for random iteration
\cite{BarnsleyElton1988}, $\mu$ is the distribution of the random series
$Z = \sum_{k\geq 1} \varepsilon_k a^k$ with
$a = (1-i)/2$ and $\varepsilon_k \overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} \{\pm 1\}$.
Since $1-a^2 = (2+i)/2$ and $\kappa=1$ (real translations), the ellipticity is
$\rho = (1-|a|^2)\kappa/|1-a^2|
= 1/\sqrt{5}$,
where $5 = \mathbf{N}(2+i)$ is the norm of the Gaussian prime $2+i$.
Thus $\varphi$ enters via the Gaussian factorisation $5=(2+i)(2-i)$
in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, and the identity $\mathrm{AR}^2 = (1-\rho)/(1+\rho)$
from~\eqref{eq:family} immediately gives $1/\varphi^2$
(using $\varphi^2=\varphi+1$).
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Further directions}

An explicit closed-form construction of a CRS
achieving $\kappa=\kappa_m^*$ for all~$N$ would be desirable;
this amounts to a system of quadratic Diophantine equations
in the digit shifts $c_k\in\mathbb{Z}[i]$
(where $d_k = k + c_k\lambda$), which does not appear to simplify
into a universal formula.
The sign-flip extension of Remark~\ref{rem:sign-flip} provides an
additional degree of freedom: by choosing
$\varepsilon_k\in\{+1,-1\}$ one can tune~$\tilde\kappa$ away
from~$\kappa$ and thereby reach aspect ratios inaccessible to the
original IFS with uniform signs.
